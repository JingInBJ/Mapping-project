China’s looming population crisis – Not alone in East Asia

Policy allowing second child receives cold feedback.

Jing Yang

Published 2019 October

After China finally lifted the controversial birth control policy in 2016, unlike the government expected, the birth rate remains calm to the encouragement.

It’s not alone in East Asia. Except to Japan, which has been on the forefront of the world’s aging trend, South Korea, reports a lowest birth rate in 2018 – 0.98, indicating the country has been in population reduction.

China -- Not rich enough to be old

Compared with its neighbors, a looming population crisis may be more frustrating for China because it’s not as rich as the other two countries when faced with the demographic pressure.

Although Trump administration takes the world’s second economy as a major competitor, as China always claims, it’s still a developing country regards its GDP per capita.

"Children? I cannot afford to raise."

The diving birth rate is unusual for this region. Under the influence of Confucianism, the three east Asian countries are supposed to be conservative with traditional family model – marriage is a must and women are supposed to be mothers.

But now, people are reluctant to do so.

A survey made in China unfolded the reason – it’s just too much cost. According to the multiple choices survey made in 2017 asking reasons for reluctant to have children, 72.1% of more than 40,000 participants said the cost, together the skyrocketing raising cost and the property cost, is the biggest obstacle.

About 42% of participants said they don’t have enough time or energy and 35% said that they are worried about the negative impacts of having children on their career development.
